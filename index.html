Abstract

Achieving simultaneous safety and positional accuracy during physical human-robot interaction remains a fundamental challenge for humanoid arms. Due to their kinematic redundancy and operation in dense, unstructured environments, these systems are highly susceptible to unexpected collisions. Furthermore, classical impedance control frameworks applied to such complex mechanisms exhibit an inherent trade-off between tracking precision and compliance, typically necessitating the integration of costly force sensors. This paper introduces a novel Fuzzy-based Adaptive Impedance Control (FAIC) framework designed to overcome the traditional accuracy-compliance barrier, delivering a dynamically adaptive interaction strategy that is strictly force-sensor-free. Central to this approach is the integration of a Generalized Momentum Observer (GMO) with a lightweight fuzzy logic mechanism. Instead of relying on direct hardware measurements, the framework employs estimated forces from the GMO to drive a fuzzy inference system, enabling the real-time modulation of impedance parameters. This mechanism maintains high stiffness for precise trajectory tracking in free space while actively transitioning to a highly compliant state upon physical contact, thereby mitigating collision impacts in redundant structures. Validated through simulations and experiments on a physical 7-DOF humanoid manipulator, the proposed framework successfully eliminates the need for direct force sensing. Results demonstrate that FAIC significantly reduces interaction energy and contact forces compared to position control methods, confirming it as a highly effective solution for safe, robust humanoid robotics applications.
